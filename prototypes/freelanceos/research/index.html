  3. 03

    Payment flow mapping

    Synthesis doc

    Mapped how money actually moves - from finished work through invoicing, reminders, bank arrival, and reconciliation. Turned scattered survey stories into a shared flow the team could design against.

    Freelancer payment workflow, from project completion to money received

    Step What happens Jobs-to-be-done
    1. Mark work as completed The freelancer finishes a task, milestone, project phase, or monthly retainer period. Know what work is ready to bill for. Avoid forgetting completed work. Separate “done” work from “still in progress.”
    2. Confirm client approval The client approves the work, either formally or informally through email, WhatsApp, Slack, etc. Capture proof of approval. Understand whether payment can be requested. Avoid billing too early or missing approval signals.
    3. Check scope, hours, and agreed terms The freelancer checks what was agreed: fixed fee, hourly rate, milestone, revisions, expenses, VAT, payment terms. Make sure the invoice amount is correct. Avoid undercharging. Identify extra work that should be billed.
    4. Prepare invoice details Client details, project name, line items, tax/VAT info, due date, payment method, invoice number. Generate an accurate invoice quickly. Reduce admin work. Keep invoice records consistent.
    5. Send invoice or payment request The invoice is sent through email, accounting software, client portal, or chat. Deliver the payment request in the right channel. Make sure the client received it. Track when it was sent.
    6. Track invoice status The freelancer waits for signs that the invoice was opened, acknowledged, approved internally, or ignored. Know whether to wait or follow up. Avoid manually searching email and chats. Reduce anxiety around “did they see it?”
    7. Estimate expected cash flow The freelancer predicts when the money should arrive based on payment terms, client habits, and past payments. Understand short-term income. Plan bills, savings, taxes, and runway. Know which payments are reliable or risky.
    8. Follow up on unpaid invoices If payment is late or close to due, the freelancer sends reminders. Chase payments without feeling awkward. Use the right tone. Prioritize the invoices that matter most.
    9. Detect payment received Money lands in the connected bank account, payment platform, or wallet. Know immediately that payment arrived. Match incoming money to the right invoice, client, or project.
    10. Reconcile invoice and bank transaction The dashboard links the paid invoice with the actual bank deposit, including partial payments, fees, currency conversion, or late payments. Confirm what was actually paid. Spot missing amounts. Keep records clean for accounting and taxes.
    11. Update income, taxes, and financial view The payment becomes part of monthly income, taxable income, cash flow, and client performance data. Understand net earnings, not just gross revenue. Prepare for taxes. See whether the month is on track.
    12. Learn from the client or project The freelancer evaluates payment speed, profitability, effort, scope creep, and repeat potential. Identify best clients. Decide who to prioritize, raise rates for, avoid, or follow up with for more work.
  4. 04

    Theme generation

    Synthesis doc

    Pulled recurring ideas into broader themes - the emotional weight of chasing payments, fragmented tools, cash-flow anxiety, and the wish for something automatic but gentle. These became the north stars for product and visual direction.

    Top themes

    Rank Theme Why it matters Product implication
    1 Payment certainty Freelancers care less about “revenue analytics” and more about knowing whether money is actually coming. PayPal reported that half of U.S. freelancers surveyed had experienced not being paid. Make expected / paid / overdue status central.
    2 Late payments and chasing clients Late payment is a recurring pain. YunoJuno research reported 41% of UK freelancers are consistently paid late, with the average late invoice overdue by about two and a half weeks. Surface overdue invoices, reminders, and “next follow-up” actions.
    3 Cash-flow visibility The key anxiety is not only “how much did I earn?” but “can I plan my month?” Intuit reported 61% of small businesses struggle with cash flow. Dashboard should show money in now, money expected, and risk gaps.
    4 Fragmented workflow Freelancers often jump between invoices, banking apps, email, spreadsheets, and accounting tools. Reviews of FreshBooks and Bonsai praise centralizing invoices, payments, contracts, reminders, and financial visibility in one place. Position the dashboard as a single source of truth, not just another reporting screen.
    5 Professionalism with clients PayPal’s freelancer report says 44% believed nonpayment happens because freelancers are not taken seriously, and recommends invoicing software because it saves time and positions freelancers as legitimate businesses. Include polished invoice/payment status, client records, and clear payment terms.
    6 Low-friction reminders Chasing payment is emotionally awkward. Many business owners feel uncomfortable chasing payments because they fear sounding rude or upsetting customers. Automate reminders, but keep tone controlled and relationship-safe.
    7 Knowing which clients are worth it Freelancers do not only need income totals. They need to know which clients pay reliably, pay well, and create less admin burden. Add a “top clients” view with revenue + payment reliability, not just revenue.
  5. 05

    Baseline prompts

    ChatGPT Cursor

    Wrote two anchor prompts before touching pixels: a solution baseline (what the product should do and feel like) and a design baseline (visual tone, density, and interaction principles). These kept later iterations aligned with research instead of drifting into generic dashboard patterns.

    • ·Solution baseline - scope, jobs-to-be-done, success criteria
    • ·Design baseline - calm Apple-inspired system, typography, component behavior

Product focus

Research surfaced a wide problem space. The prototype deliberately narrows to the stretch after work is done - when freelancers need to see what they've earned and shepherd payments in, without becoming accountants.
